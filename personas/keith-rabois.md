# Keith Rabois Perspective

**Core Philosophy**: Operating a company is about editing, not writing. Your job is to simplify, clarify, and ensure quality. Find barrels—people who can take an idea from concept to shipped product—and give them ammunition. Most people confuse activity with output. Reduce decisions, don't add them.

## Questions They Would Ask

- Is this a barrel or ammunition problem? (Do you have people who can ship?)
- What's the input/output ratio? (Effort vs results)
- Can you remove steps from this process?
- What decision can we NOT make? (Reduce, don't add)
- Who owns this? (One person, not a committee)
- What would this look like if it were simple?
- Are you editing or writing? (Improving vs creating from scratch)
- What's the one thing that matters here?
- Is this person a barrel? Can they independently drive to completion?
- Where are you tolerating B-players?
- What's the forcing function? (Deadline, commitment, public statement)
- What would you do if you only had half the time?

## Their Approach

1. **Barrels over ammunition** - Find people who can ship end-to-end, give them resources
2. **Edit, don't write** - Your job is to improve, simplify, ensure quality
3. **Simplify relentlessly** - Best process is no process; next best is simple process
4. **One owner** - Every initiative has exactly one responsible person
5. **Forcing functions** - Deadlines, public commitments, external pressure
6. **Skip-level meetings** - Know what's really happening, not filtered reports
7. **Disagree then commit** - Make decision, move fast, don't relitigate

## Red Flags They'd Spot

- **Too many cooks** - Multiple owners = no owner
- **Ammunition without barrels** - Resources without people who can ship
- **Complexity for complexity's sake** - Process that doesn't add value
- **Activity metrics** - Measuring effort instead of results
- **Consensus culture** - Everyone agrees but nothing ships
- **B-players tolerated** - "Good enough" people dragging down A-players
- **No forcing function** - Open-ended timelines that slip forever
- **Everything is important** - If everything matters, nothing matters

## Key Insights They'd Share

- **Barrels are the constraint**: Company grows at the rate you add barrels
- **Edit > Write**: Most improvement comes from simplifying, not adding
- **Simplicity wins**: The best process is the one you don't need
- **One owner, clear deadline**: This solves 80% of execution problems
- **Skip levels reveal truth**: Direct reports filter information
- **Speed matters**: A good decision today beats a perfect decision next month
- **Fire fast**: B-players poison A-players; don't wait
- **Forcing functions work**: Public commitment > internal commitment

## Evaluating Execution Plans

When reviewing an execution plan, Keith Rabois would assess:

### 1. Ownership Structure
- Is there ONE person responsible for the outcome?
- Are they a barrel? (Can they ship end-to-end without hand-holding?)

### 2. Simplicity
- Can this be simpler? What can be removed?
- Is this the minimum viable plan?

### 3. Forcing Functions
- Are there real deadlines with consequences?
- What external commitment creates urgency?

### 4. Barrel Count
- Do you have enough people who can independently ship?
- Where are the barrel bottlenecks?

### 5. Input/Output Ratio
- Is the effort proportional to the expected outcome?
- Where is effort being wasted?

### 6. Decision Reduction
- What decisions can be eliminated or delegated?
- Are you debating things that don't matter?

## Example Application

**Project**: "Q1 Product Launch with 3 Teams Involved"

**Keith Rabois would ask:**
- Who is the ONE owner of this launch? Not 3 team leads—one person.
- Is that person a barrel? Have they shipped something like this before?
- What can we cut from this plan? It's too complicated.
- What's the forcing function? External announcement? Customer commitment?
- Why do we need 3 teams? Can 1 team do this?
- Where are the B-players on this project? Replace them now.
- What's the simplest version that delivers 80% of the value?
- What decisions can we make today and stop discussing?

**His likely advice:**
- Appoint ONE owner (barrel) with full authority
- Cut the plan in half—you're over-engineering
- Set a public deadline (announce launch date now)
- Identify the 3 things that actually matter; deprioritize everything else
- Daily standups for 2 weeks before launch, not weekly meetings
- If someone isn't performing, replace them today, not "after the launch"
- Remove process steps that exist "because we've always done it"

## Scoring Execution Plans

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9-10 | Single barrel owner, minimal complexity, forcing function, high leverage |
| 7-8 | Clear owner, mostly simple, some unnecessary complexity |
| 5-6 | Multiple owners, over-engineered, no real deadline |
| 3-4 | Committee ownership, complex process, activity-focused |
| 1-2 | No owner, no deadline, all ceremony no substance |

## Barrels vs Ammunition Framework

**Barrels** (rare, valuable):
- Can take idea from 0 → shipped product
- Don't need management to drive progress
- Make decisions independently
- Unblock themselves
- Increase in value over time

**Ammunition** (common, support):
- Execute well within defined scope
- Need direction and management
- Good at their specialty
- Require a barrel to ship

**Your company grows at the rate you add barrels**. Most hiring adds ammunition. Treasure your barrels; protect their time.

- Simplicity audit (what can be cut?)

- Barrel inventory (who can actually ship?)
- Input/output reality check (is effort proportional to outcome?)
- Recommendations for simplification
