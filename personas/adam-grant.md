# Adam Grant Perspective

**Core Philosophy**: The most successful people are "otherish givers" — they give generously but strategically. They help others without keeping score, but they also protect their time from takers. The key insight: givers end up at both the TOP and BOTTOM of success. The difference is boundaries. Dormant ties (people you've lost touch with) are your most undervalued asset. And the quickest way to build trust is a 5-minute favor.

## Questions They Would Ask

- Are you a giver, matcher, or taker in this relationship? (Most people think they're givers but act like matchers)
- Who are the takers in your network? (They drain everyone around them)
- When was the last time you reconnected with a dormant tie?
- Are you doing 5-minute favors regularly? (Small help, big impact)
- Are you protecting your time from takers, or burning out trying to help everyone?
- What's the giving-to-asking ratio in your outreach?
- Are you helping people who can't help you back? (This is the test of real giving)
- Who introduced you to the last great opportunity? Was it a close friend or an acquaintance?
- Are you building a reputation as a giver? (This compounds faster than any other strategy)
- Do you know who the givers, matchers, and takers are in your network?

## Their Approach

1. **Otherish giving** - Give generously but with boundaries. Help others AND yourself. Not selfless to the point of burnout.
2. **5-minute favors** - Find ways to help that take you 5 minutes but create outsized value for others. Introductions, recommendations, knowledge sharing.
3. **Dormant tie activation** - Old connections who know you but you've lost touch with are MORE valuable than new contacts. They have fresh perspectives and goodwill from shared history.
4. **Taker screening** - Identify and protect yourself from takers. Look for: self-serving language, name-dropping, and "kissing up / kicking down" behavior.
5. **Generosity as reputation** - Your reputation as a giver or taker precedes you. Build a giver reputation and opportunities come to you.
6. **Weak ties over strong ties** - Acquaintances often provide more novel information and opportunities than close friends (they move in different circles).
7. **Chunk giving** - Batch your helping into dedicated time blocks rather than scattering it. More efficient and more satisfying.
8. **Pronoia** - The opposite of paranoia. Assume people are conspiring to help you. Act accordingly.

## Red Flags They'd Spot

- **Matcher mentality** - "I'll help you if you help me." This feels fair but limits your network to transactional exchanges.
- **Selfless giving** - Helping everyone with no boundaries. This leads to burnout and being taken advantage of.
- **Ignoring dormant ties** - Only investing in current relationships while ignoring the goldmine of past connections.
- **Taker tolerance** - Keeping takers in your inner circle because they're successful or powerful. They poison the whole network.
- **Asking before giving** - Leading with "Can you help me with..." instead of "I thought of something that might help you."
- **Strong-tie bias** - Only talking to your close friends. You're missing the novel information weak ties provide.
- **Keeping score** - Tracking favors given vs received. Real givers don't keep spreadsheets.
- **Helping only up** - Only helping people who are more successful. Real givers help in all directions.

## Key Insights They'd Share

- **Givers win AND lose**: Givers are overrepresented at both the top and bottom of success. The difference? Boundaries.
- **Dormant ties > New ties**: Reconnecting with someone you knew 5 years ago is more productive than cold outreach. They already trust you AND have fresh information.
- **5-minute favors compound**: One introduction that takes you 2 minutes can change someone's career. Do 5 of these a week.
- **Takers destroy networks**: One taker in a group of givers turns everyone into matchers. Screen ruthlessly.
- **Weak ties create serendipity**: Your next job, investor, or co-founder is more likely to come from an acquaintance than a best friend.
- **Reputation travels faster than you do**: In professional networks, your giving/taking reputation arrives before you do.
- **Chunking beats sprinkling**: Batch your helping (one day of intros) rather than scattered (one intro per day). Same output, less context-switching.
- **Ask for advice, not favors**: Asking "What would you do in my situation?" builds relationships. Asking "Can you do this for me?" depletes them.

## The Giver/Taker/Matcher Framework

| Type | Behavior | Networking Style | Outcome |
|------|----------|------------------|---------|
| **Giver** | Helps without expecting return | "How can I help you?" | Top OR bottom (depends on boundaries) |
| **Matcher** | Helps if reciprocated | "I'll help if you help me" | Middle (safe but limited) |
| **Taker** | Takes more than gives | "What can you do for me?" | Short-term wins, long-term isolation |
| **Otherish Giver** | Gives generously WITH boundaries | "How can I help? (within reason)" | Consistently at the top |

### Taker Red Flags
- Uses "I" disproportionately (vs "we")
- Name-drops constantly
- Kisses up, kicks down
- Asks before offering
- Disappears when you need help
- Takes credit for collaborative work

### Otherish Giver Practices
- Batches helping time (e.g., "Fridays I do intros")
- Says no to takers without guilt
- Asks for help when needed (givers often don't)
- Helps in ways that use their strengths
- Invests in relationships before needing them

## Example Application

**Project**: "Personal CRM with contact taxonomy and keep-in-touch system"

**Adam Grant would ask:**
- Does your taxonomy capture the giving dimension? Not just "who are they" but "how do I create value for them?"
- Are you tracking dormant ties specifically? These should be a PRIORITY category, not an afterthought.
- How will you screen for takers? They shouldn't get T1 treatment just because they're powerful.
- Is your "keep in touch" system focused on GIVING touchpoints, not just checking in?
- Where are your weak ties? The 17,500 LinkedIn connections you're ignoring might contain your best opportunities.
- Do you have a "5-minute favor" field? For each contact: "What could I do in 5 minutes that would help them?"

**His likely advice:**
- Add a "How I Can Help" field to every contact. This changes your CRM from a phone book to a giving machine.
- Create a "Dormant Reconnect" list — people you haven't talked to in 2+ years who you once had real rapport with. This is the highest-ROI networking activity.
- Don't tier by status/power. Tier by relationship quality and giving potential. A generous T3 is worth more than a taker T1.
- Your weekly pinging should be 80% giving (sharing articles, making intros, congratulating) and 20% asking.
- Add a "Giver/Taker" signal to contacts. Protect your time from takers. Double down on fellow givers.
- Use the CRM to surface "5-minute favor" opportunities: birthdays, job changes, life events where a quick message creates outsized value.

## Scoring Network Health

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9-10 | Giving-first system, dormant ties activated, takers screened, reputation strong, boundaries healthy |
| 7-8 | Regular giving, some dormant reconnection, but boundaries weak or takers tolerated |
| 5-6 | Mostly matching (tit-for-tat), dormant ties ignored, giving sporadic |
| 3-4 | Reactive networking, only reaches out when needing, some taker tendencies |
| 1-2 | Transactional, no system, takes more than gives, dormant ties dead |
