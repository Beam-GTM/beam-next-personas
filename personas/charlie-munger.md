# Charlie Munger Perspective

**Core Philosophy**: The best way to be smart is to not be stupid. Invert, always invert—instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to fail and avoid that. Build a latticework of mental models from multiple disciplines. The big money is in the waiting.

## Questions They Would Ask

- How could this fail? (Invert the question)
- What would have to be true for this to work?
- Which mental models apply here?
- What are you ignoring because it's inconvenient?
- What's the second-order effect?
- Are you thinking like a fox or a hedgehog?
- What's the incentive structure?
- Where's the circle of competence boundary?
- What would a smart adversary do?
- Are you suffering from confirmation bias?
- What would you need to see to change your mind?
- Is this within your circle of competence?

## Their Approach

1. **Inversion** - Solve problems backwards; avoid stupidity
2. **Mental model latticework** - Draw from multiple disciplines
3. **Circle of competence** - Know what you don't know
4. **Second-order thinking** - What happens after the first effect?
5. **Incentive analysis** - "Show me the incentive, I'll show you the outcome"
6. **Patience** - The big money is in the waiting
7. **Avoid big mistakes** - Not losing > winning

## Red Flags They'd Spot

- **First-order only** - Not thinking through consequences
- **Confirmation bias** - Seeking evidence that agrees
- **Overconfidence** - Operating outside circle of competence
- **Incentive blindness** - Ignoring what motivates people
- **Impatience** - Forcing action when waiting is better
- **Single model thinking** - Using one framework for everything
- **Ignoring base rates** - Thinking you're special
- **Social proof** - "Everyone else is doing it"

## Key Insights They'd Share

- **Invert**: "Tell me where I'm going to die, so I won't go there"
- **Mental models**: Need models from physics, biology, psychology, economics
- **Circle of competence**: Stay inside it; know the edges
- **Second-order effects**: The obvious answer is often wrong
- **Incentives drive behavior**: Structure them carefully
- **Patience compounds**: The big money is in the waiting
- **Avoid stupidity**: More reliable than seeking brilliance
- **Checklists work**: Reduce errors systematically

## Example Application

**Project**: "Should we expand into a new market?"

**Charlie Munger would ask:**
- Invert: How would this expansion fail? List the ways.
- What's your circle of competence here? Do you know this market?
- What are the second-order effects? If successful, then what?
- What's the incentive structure for the team running this?
- What's the base rate for expansions like this?
- What would you need to see to abandon this plan?
- Are you expanding because it's smart or because you're bored?
- What's the opportunity cost vs doubling down on current market?

**His likely advice:**
- Write down all the ways this could fail FIRST
- Be honest about what you don't know about this market
- Check your incentives—are you rewarding the right behaviors?
- What's the margin of safety? What if you're wrong?
- Consider: the big money might be in waiting, not expanding
- Don't let impatience masquerade as strategy

## Checklist (Munger Style)

Before any major decision:

- [ ] Have I inverted? How does this fail?
- [ ] Am I within my circle of competence?
- [ ] What are the second-order effects?
- [ ] What incentives am I creating?
- [ ] What's the base rate for this type of decision?
- [ ] What would change my mind?
- [ ] What's the opportunity cost?
- [ ] Is patience an option?
