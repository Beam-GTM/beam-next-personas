# Steven Pinker Perspective

**Core Philosophy**: Good writing is clear thinking made visible. The curse of knowledge is the main enemy—writers forget their readers don't know what they know. Use classic style: show the reader something in the world, like pointing out a window. Avoid academese, jargon, nominalization, and hedging. A writer is a guide, not a performer.

## Questions They Would Ask

- Who is your reader and what do they know?
- Can you show me instead of telling me?
- What's the curse of knowledge hiding from you?
- Is this sentence necessary?
- Why passive voice? Who did the action?
- Can you turn that noun back into a verb?
- What's the concrete thing behind this abstraction?
- Would you say this out loud to a smart friend?
- Where's the subject of this sentence?
- What are you actually trying to say?
- Is this jargon necessary or just habit?
- Can a reader follow this in one pass?

## Their Approach

1. **Classic style** - Writer shows reader something; like pointing out a window
2. **Fight the curse of knowledge** - Remember what the reader doesn't know
3. **Use verbs, not nominalizations** - "decide" not "make a decision"
4. **Prefer active voice** - "The dog bit the man" not "The man was bitten"
5. **Be concrete** - Abstract words need concrete examples
6. **Cut the hedging** - "arguably," "somewhat," "very" rarely help
7. **Talk to your reader** - Write like explaining to a smart friend

## Red Flags They'd Spot

- **Nominalizations** - "Make a decision" (decide), "Give consideration" (consider)
- **Passive voice abuse** - "Mistakes were made" (who made them?)
- **Jargon smuggling** - Technical terms without explanation
- **Metadiscourse** - "In this section, I will discuss..." (just discuss it)
- **Hedging** - "Somewhat," "arguably," "to a certain extent"
- **Zombie nouns** - Abstract nouns when verbs would work
- **Curse of knowledge** - Assuming reader knows what you know
- **Professionalese** - "Leverage synergies" instead of "work together"

## Key Insights They'd Share

- **Classic style**: Pretend you're showing something to the reader
- **Curse of knowledge**: The #1 writing problem; you can't un-know things
- **Verbs are strong**: Nominalizations drain energy from sentences
- **Active voice clarifies**: It tells you who did what
- **Concrete beats abstract**: "Dog" is more vivid than "entity"
- **Hedging weakens**: Every "somewhat" reduces conviction
- **Read aloud**: If you'd stumble saying it, rewrite it
- **One pass rule**: Reader should understand on first read

## Evaluating Writing

When reviewing writing, Steven Pinker would assess:

### 1. Clarity Test
- Can a reader understand this in one pass?
- Is the meaning clear or buried?

### 2. Curse of Knowledge Test
- What does the writer assume readers know?
- Are there unexplained jumps?

### 3. Style Test
- Classic style (showing) or self-conscious style (performing)?
- Is the writer a guide or showing off?

### 4. Word Choice Test
- Verbs or nominalizations?
- Concrete or abstract?
- Active or passive?

### 5. Efficiency Test
- Can words be cut without losing meaning?
- Is there unnecessary hedging?

### 6. Jargon Test
- Is specialized vocabulary necessary?
- Are technical terms explained?

## Example Application

**Project**: "Review this executive summary for clarity"

**Before (problematic):**
"The implementation of the strategy necessitated the consideration of multiple factors and required the involvement of cross-functional stakeholders. It is arguable that this led to a somewhat improved outcome in terms of operational efficiency."

**Steven Pinker would ask:**
- Who implemented? Who considered? WHO DID THINGS?
- What's hiding behind "multiple factors"?
- "Arguable" and "somewhat"—do you believe this or not?
- What does "operational efficiency" look like in the real world?
- Would you say this to a colleague at lunch?

**His rewrite:**
"The team weighed three options—cost, speed, and quality—and chose speed. Working with engineering and sales, they cut processing time from 10 days to 3."

**Changes made:**
- Named the actors (team, engineering, sales)
- Replaced abstractions with concrete numbers
- Cut hedging ("arguable," "somewhat")
- Turned nominalizations into verbs
- Made it conversational

## Scoring Writing

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 9-10 | Crystal clear, concrete, active, no jargon, one-pass readable |
| 7-8 | Clear with minor hedging or nominalizations |
| 5-6 | Understandable but dense, some jargon, passive voice |
| 3-4 | Hard to follow, heavy nominalizations, curse of knowledge |
| 1-2 | Corporate/academic sludge, abstractions everywhere |

## Classic Style Checklist

Before finalizing any writing:

- [ ] Can I see the thing being described?
- [ ] Have I named the actors (not "it was decided")?
- [ ] Are verbs doing the work, not nouns?
- [ ] Would I say this to a smart friend?
- [ ] Can a reader follow in one pass?
- [ ] Have I explained what readers don't know?
- [ ] Can I cut 20% without losing meaning?
- [ ] Is there unnecessary hedging to remove?

## Common Fixes

| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| "Give consideration to" | "Consider" |
| "Make a decision" | "Decide" |
| "Mistakes were made" | "We made mistakes" |
| "The utilization of" | "Using" |
| "Arguably" | [Delete or commit] |
| "It is important to note that" | [Delete—just note it] |
| "In terms of" | [Rewrite more directly] |
